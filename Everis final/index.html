Part 00

How to use this

A working system for Everis itself and the products Everis acquires. Read it straight through once. After that, jump to the section the situation actually needs.

00
00 Entry points

The product has 340 paying customers. The founder is already thinking about the next thing. The welcome email still says "explore the dashboard." The Zapier page has not been touched in years. The top review compares it to a competitor that no longer exists.

I have 90 days before anyone asks whether growth is happening. This is how I would spend them.

This playbook is built for Everis's actual shape of business. Small software products. Lean teams. B2B SaaS, workflow tools, and the occasional mobile app. Products with real utility, weak positioning, and more upside in distribution than in reinvention.

It does two jobs. First, it covers how I would audit, position, and grow the products Everis acquires. Second, it covers how I would help Everis market itself well enough to attract the right founders, the right operators, and the right reputation over time.

Part 01

Diagnose before you treat

The first ten minutes matter. I need to understand the product's actual condition before developing any acquisition theory.

01
01 First read

The first thing I would check is the support inbox. That tells you more in ten minutes than a handover deck ever will.

If the inbox is full, if customers are following up on old tickets, if long-tenure users are sending careful, worried emails, don't touch the growth levers yet. First, prioritise how to stop the bleeding. Stability. The product may still be good. But it was being held together by one person's daily attention, and that attention is gone.

If the inbox is clean and the founder is helpful, clear, and already excited about what comes next, that is different. The product is usually intact. What ran out was energy, not viability.

That distinction matters because it tells me whether month one is defensive or offensive.

Burnout

Stabilise first

Support backlog. Stale docs. Quiet churn. Before campaigns. First move: clear the inbox, build/restore trust, find out who's halfway out the door. Everything else comes after.

New venture

Extract everything

The founder is useful now and far less useful in three weeks. This is the profile where market memory disappears fastest if nobody captures it.

Bored or pivoting

Find the old growth engine

The product usually still works. Distribution lost momentum first. The real question is whether the channel belonged to the product or the founder.

Clean exit

Audit, then move

Healthiest handover. Best chance of getting through the audit quickly and moving into actual growth posture without spending month one in recovery mode.

I've seen people misread the healthy-looking product most often. The UI is tidy, the founder sounds organised, and the numbers look stable enough. Then month two arrives and nothing moves because the only real channel was the founder's personal audience.

Founders describe exits kindly. Products do not. I would trust the inbox, the billing data, the reviews, and the cohort curve before I trust the seller's summary of what happened.

Part 02

The audit

Week one to two. No campaigns. No redesign. No channel bets. I want the truth before I want the plan.

Pull every review and copy the exact language

G2. Capterra. Trustpilot. App Store. Anywhere the product is discussed in public. I want the sentences where customers describe the product in their own words. Not the polished version. Their version.

That becomes the raw material for positioning, lifecycle copy, onboarding fixes, homepage edits, and future campaigns. I read early reviews separately from recent ones. Early reviews usually hold the original reason the product won. Recent reviews often show loyalty without precision.

Find out where paying customers actually came from

Not traffic. Paid customers. I want the source of trial signups, the source of paid conversions, and the specific content or moment that produced them. In most acquired products, the highest-traffic channel is not the highest-quality one.

I also want to know how much of that channel belonged to the founder personally. Newsletter. Personal brand. Community standing. Podcast appearances. If the channel was the founder, the channel did not transfer.

Check timezone distribution before scheduling anything. I have done the stupid version of this. A campaign that looked fine in one timezone landed in half the inboxes while people were asleep.

Part 03

Positioning

The website is not the positioning. The tagline is not the positioning. The positioning is the claim that makes the right person slow down.

03
03 Value proposition

Most founders can tell you what the product does. That is not the same as telling you why the best customers chose it.

The real value prop lives in the switch. What did the customer stop doing when they adopted this? What did it replace? What headache disappeared? What old process became unnecessary? That is the useful question.

The real value prop lives in the switch, not the feature list.

The word-bank method

I build a word-bank from reviews, support praise, customer emails, and handover notes. I look for repeated phrases that customers arrived at on their own.

"The first one that did not take a week to set up." "Built for a team our size." "Finally replaced the spreadsheet." That language carries weight because nobody coached it.

The first time I did this properly it took two hours. The second time it took less. The output barely changed. The value is in reading carefully, not in making the method look sophisticated.

The replacement framing

Feature-based positioning is usually too abstract. Replacement-based positioning is usually closer to the buying decision. A product that replaces a spreadsheet should not sound like a giant platform. A product that replaces three stitched-together tools should say so plainly.

The compass sentence

Once the word-bank is clear, I want one internal sentence. For this specific customer, in this specific situation, this product is the fastest or simplest or clearest way to get this outcome instead of the messy thing they are doing now. Not tagline copy. A compass.

Before
Powerful project management for modern teams.
After
For agency ops leads still running projects out of spreadsheets and Slack, the project tool that gets usable in an afternoon.
Part 04

Channel selection

I do not start with channel preferences. I start with buying behavior, product shape, and whether the old growth engine belonged to the product or the founder.

04
04 Decision frame

There are only a few questions that matter. Do buyers search for this type of product by category? Do they ask peers instead? Is the ACV low enough that this has to be inbound? Is this a SaaS discovery problem or a mobile app discovery problem?

Low ACV
Higher ACV
Searching ICP
SEO, GEO, review categories

Most of Everis's portfolio will live here. Compound channels first.

Search plus authority content

Case studies, evaluation pages, category depth, and a stronger sales-assist layer.

Non-searching ICP
Community, integrations, referral later

Useful where the problem is discussed before the tool is searched for.

Partnerships and direct relationship channels

Events, co-marketing, partner distribution, targeted outbound.

The channel hierarchy for Everis's typical acquisition

  • Email first. Annual plan offer, welcome email rewrite, dead trial reactivation. Fastest feedback, lowest lift, closest to revenue.
  • Review velocity second. G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, App Store. Not vanity. Conversion signal and AI citation fuel.
  • Search and GEO next. Category discovery still matters, and AI assistants now shape a share of that discovery.
  • One new channel after that. One real experiment with one real hypothesis.
  • Paid last. Because paid amplifies broken conversion faster than it creates growth.

On GEO in 2026

Traditional SEO still matters. But if a buyer asks an AI assistant what tool to use and the product never appears, that is now a distribution gap worth fixing in the first 90 days.

The first time I ran the AI visibility audit on a newly acquired product, the product did not appear in any of the four tools I checked. Its supposed competitor appeared in all of them. That is not a future problem. That is a current one.

Part 05

The 90 day experiment

Not a campaign calendar. Not a pile of activity. A hypothesis, a metric, and a decision threshold set before launch.

05
05 Hypothesis

If I cannot write the hypothesis sentence, I am not ready to run the test.

We believe this customer discovers this product in this way when this trigger happens. We will test it by doing this. We will define success as this metric reaching this number by this date.

That forces clarity. "Let's try content" does not fit that sentence. A real experiment does.

Weeks 1 and 2

Audit only. No experiments before the product's reality is clear.

Weeks 3 and 4

Positioning refined. Core message chosen. Hypothesis written.

Month 2

Run the first real test. Track signal weekly, not vaguely.

Day 60

Scale it, adjust it, or kill it. No fuzzy middle.

What Everis products typically need first

At Everis's typical product profile, B2B SaaS, underbuilt marketing infrastructure, modest ARR, a lean team, the sequence is usually the same. Month one is email and conversion clean-up. Month two is searchable content plus review velocity. Month three is the first paid or community test if the numbers support it.

30

60

90

I have seen month three turn into a vague conversation about whether something "felt promising." That is what happens when the threshold was never defined at the start. I want to avoid those vague conversations.

Part 06

Days 3 to 30

Month one is where quiet decay either continues or gets interrupted. This is where we take action. Not theory. Execution.

06
06 First month

Month one is where weak products keep quietly decaying if nobody pays attention. This is the part that should feel most usable.

Annual plan conversion

The fastest cash-flow and retention move in many small SaaS products is simple. Add the annual option, price it cleanly, and tell monthly customers it exists. Not clever. Just underused.

Welcome email rewrite

The welcome email is usually the most-read message in the whole product and the worst-written one. I would rewrite it around one outcome and one next step. Not "explore the dashboard." One useful action.

Old welcome email
We're excited to have you on board. Explore the dashboard and let us know if you need any help getting started.
What I would send
You signed up because something in your workflow is not working. The fastest next step is this one. It takes two minutes. What were you trying to fix when you found us?

Dead trial reactivation

This is the skipped move. Not because people do not know about it. Because it feels awkward to email people who vanished. I would rather have that awkwardness than ignore a list of people who were already close enough to sign up once.

Three emails. Honest tone. One friction point per send. One specific offer in the final message.

Review velocity programme

The moment a founder leaves, the review stream usually dies. That hurts conversion, category trust, and AI visibility at once. I would restart the review loop where value is already visible.

Long-tenure customers

Every customer who has been here since the early days is both a retention risk and a reputation asset. They need a human note early, before they invent a story about what ownership change means.

By the end of week one, I want the annual offer live, the welcome email rewritten, and the list of long-tenure customers in front of me.

Part 07

Build what compounds

Quick wins buy time. They are not the system. The system is what keeps paying after the easy fixes stop being enough.

07
07 Longer game

The system is what compounds after the obvious fixes stop being enough.

NRR above 100 percent

If net revenue retention is below 100 percent, growth is partly replacing leakage. That changes how aggressively I would invest in acquisition. If Everis owns products with flat pricing, weak expansion paths, and no meaningful middle tier, that is not just a marketing issue. It is a structure issue.

113

Top quartile NRR is a very different business from the one living below 100. The gap compounds.

1

Strong integration can outperform a pile of generic content when it lands in the customer's real workflow.

4

Customer conversations a month is a better cadence than occasional research sprints followed by silence.

Integration-led distribution

For many small SaaS products, the best growth channel is not paid. It is being embedded in the workflow the customer already uses.

Community presence

I care more about where people discuss the work than where they compare tools. Customer communities matter more than vendor chatter.

Four customer interviews per month

One churned customer. One recent convert. One power user. One long-tenure account. Every month. That is the part most teams let slide, then they wonder why the copy gets vague again.

The churned customer interview is the one most people skip because it feels uncomfortable. After the first few, what replaces the discomfort is useful information.

Book four customer interviews before anything else this week. Let the next month's decisions come from those calls.

ASO is usually the missed lever

A bad app listing sounds like the founder describing what they built. A good listing sounds like the buyer describing what they are looking for. Title matters. Subtitle matters. First lines matter. Screenshots matter more than most teams admit.

If the app has not had fresh reviews in months, that matters too. Fresh reviews matter more than most people think. I'd treat this as a distribution problem, not just store cleanup.

Part 09

The portfolio layer

At product three, the model changes. I stop asking how to market one product well and start asking how to run a portfolio system without drowning in context switching.

09
09 Shared system

Standardise the stack early

  • Shared analytics logic
  • Shared email infrastructure
  • Shared review management rhythm
  • Shared naming for events and lifecycle moments

Protect comparability

  • Parallel tracking before migration
  • Preserve historical reference points
  • Document the baseline before changes
  • Avoid month four confusion masquerading as analysis

Cross-portfolio CRM

Some customers will overlap across products. Some audiences will too. That only becomes useful if someone can actually see it. This is where a holding company starts becoming smarter than a set of separate products.

Shared learning system

Every useful result should travel. Pricing test that worked. Subject line pattern that worked. Review prompt wording that worked. Integration strategy that worked. If one product learns and the others do not inherit it, the portfolio is slower than it needs to be.

One marketer, more than one product

One strong marketer can run more than one product if the system is built for it. Standard dashboards. Clean prompts. Shared experiment logs. Clear positioning memory. That is the difference between working across a portfolio and constantly reloading context from scratch.

If Everis is already running two or more products, I would create a shared marketing learnings document this week and start logging results immediately.

Part 10

How Everis markets itself

Not founder-brand content dressed up as company strategy. Everis-specific marketing. Why the right founder should think of Everis first when it is finally time to sell.

10
10 Reputation infrastructure

The question is simple. Why should a strong bootstrapped founder think of Everis first when it is finally time to sell?

The answer is not just founder credibility. That helps. It is not enough. The answer is that Everis becomes known for what it actually does after the close.

That means public proof, not vague positioning. What changed after acquisition. What stayed the same. What improved. What the founder appreciated. What the team learned. What would be done differently next time.

What I want to build here is a reputation that compounds, not just a content schedule.

The Everis narrative

It should say this plainly. We buy useful software that never reached the heights it should have. We know how to run and grow it. We respect what made it work in the first place. And we are willing to show our work.

That last point matters most. Specific proof travels farther than abstract positioning. It also ages better.

What that looks like in practice

Case-led content. Honest founder stories. Clear operational notes. Running Remote as a room where the right founders already are. My take is simple: the event does not create trust. Trust comes from people seeing that you live what you preach and from the specific proof you bring. The event just gives that proof a bigger stage. Sidebar: such a cool community.

Part 11

Anti-patterns

These are the mistakes I would expect to see again and again. Not theoretical ones. Observable ones.

11
11 What kills momentum

Running campaigns on a burnout product

More traffic into a shaky experience just creates negative sentiment faster.

Rebranding too early

A new owner does not need a new visual identity in week three. That usually signals anxiety, not strategy.

Buying traffic before fixing conversion

Paid amplifies the leak. It does not solve it.

Inherited technical debt

If the founder accumulated years of shortcuts and quick fixes, I want to know before I build more on top of fragile foundations.

Broken deliverability

A strong lifecycle sequence means nothing if the domain setup is quietly damaged.

Marketing to the founder's imagined customer

The founder built for freelancers. The money came from agencies. That happens all the time.

Referral programme too early

Do not pour more users into a bucket that is still leaking badly.

Using the handover period for the wrong questions

Technical questions matter. Market memory matters more.

Building Everis out of the founders' old stories

Time Doctor and Running Remote are credibility assets. They are not the Everis story on their own.

The biggest mistake is trying to fix everything at once. Too many changes, no sequencing, muddy measurement, false urgency. The order exists for a reason.
